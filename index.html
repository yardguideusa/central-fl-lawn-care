Central Florida Lawn Care

Sandy-soil basics for Orlando and the surrounding metro.

Lawn care in Central Florida is less about coaxing grass to grow and more about managing a yard that grows nearly year-round in fast-draining sand under intense sun and afternoon downpours. Orlando sits in USDA zone 9b, a humid subtropical climate that sees around 50 inches of rain a year — most of it in summer thunderstorms — and almost never a hard freeze.

The sand factor: Central Florida soils are sandy, low in organic matter, and drain so quickly that water and nutrients leach right past the root zone. That changes everything — you fertilize lighter and more often, and you build the soil with compost rather than assuming it holds anything.

St. Augustine country

The default lawn here is St. Augustine grass (Floratam and similar cultivars), which handles the heat and humidity well but brings its own watch-list: chinch bugs in hot dry spells, gray leaf spot in the rainy season, and sensitivity to over-fertilizing. Bahia is a tougher, lower-input alternative for large sunny lots, and zoysia is gaining ground for a finer texture.

Watering — rules and rhythm

Because the aquifer supplies the region’s drinking water, year-round landscape irrigation limits apply across Central Florida, typically restricting how many days a week and what hours you may water. Check your assigned schedule and any tighter local rules through the St. Johns River Water Management District’s watering restrictions. Counter-intuitively, the sandy soil and frequent rain mean many Orlando lawns are over-watered — deep, infrequent irrigation grows better roots than daily sprinkling.

Feeding and timing

With essentially no winter dormancy, the growing season runs long, but summer rains can wash nutrients away quickly, so slow-release fertilizer applied in the right amounts beats heavy single doses. Many Florida counties also enforce summer fertilizer blackout periods to protect waterways. For cultivar advice, pest identification, and a Central-Florida lawn calendar, the University of Florida runs a free service through UF/IFAS Extension in Orange County.
